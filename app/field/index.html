Lifeblood of the Cultivated Land

Mountain snowmelt arrives onto the plains to water thirsty fields, supporting the economy of an entire region.

“I’ll be waiting for you at seven, in my blue truck in the driveway,” instructed the kind yet gruff voice of Bob Busch on the phone. He’s prompt, and expects the same from visitors. Just past seven the next day, he directed the way to one of his sugar beet fields along the Gering/Fort Laramie Irrigation District’s canal in the cool morning breeze.

Wearing faded denim overalls, a toothy smile and a Kelly Bean Co. mesh hat, Busch watched his grandson and a farm hand “move water” with an approving gaze. He knows this process well. A third-generation farmer in the Nebraska panhandle, Busch has spent nearly all of his 77 years on the same patch of land outside of Gering. After a short stint at college, he took over farming the family property from his father in 1959.

“I dearly love farming,” Busch said. “I don’t like some of the adversity we have. Weather… You’d get a hail storm, oh, mercy. You’d be done. But farming’s my life and always has been.”

Busch’s son Kendall, 52, now runs the operation, more than 1,000 acres of corn, sugar beets, dry edible beans and alfalfa. They still use flood or furrow irrigation, an older method than ?? that requires a lot of early summer mornings.

After setting the siphon tubes, they’ll let the water flow across this field for 24 hours before moving it (what - the water or the tubes or the process?)again tomorrow morning. They do this throughout the irrigation season, from May through September, making the most of their water.

“Our [operation] is very labor intense. It takes a lot of manpower and a lot of time,” Kendall Busch said.
