Water Comes from White Wonder

It all begins up high, where snow piles in sheets, in waves, in avalanche cascades in Colorado’s Rocky Mountains.

By Ariana Brocious

scroll

The sharp peaks of the Nokhu Crags emerge slowly and gracefully above a dark ridge of pine on the steep climb to Lake Agnes. The trail ends on a rocky outcropping above a lake of black glass, where the sides of snowy mountains slide down to the shore. Winter winds whip around this remote alpine spot, the birthplace of the North Platte River.

As it has for centuries, water arrives annually to these mountains as snow and ice, collecting at high elevations to wait for spring’s warmth. In the arid west, the majority of the water that fills streams and rivers comes from winter snowpack. Every year, farmers, raft guides, fishermen and others wait anxiously to see what winter will bring.

Ski fanatics know exactly how much fresh powder each storm brings to their mountains. But others track that data, too. For the last 100 years, this is when the cycle of water management begins, as hydrologists answer a critical question: how much water is up there this year?

Mage Hultstrand glides through deep snow, approaching a collection of silvery devices coated in icy powder. “We measure the water content of the snow here,” says Hultstrand, a hydrologist with the Natural Resources Conservation Service’s snow survey program in Denver. She’s at the Joe Wright SNOTEL site in the Cache la Poudre drainage, part of the South Platte River Basin.

Mage explains a SNOTEL site.

She explainsexplains each of the snow-covered instruments, including a snow “pillow,” full of an antifreeze solution. It acts like a liquid scale, measuring the weight of the snow and converting it to total water content of the snowpack. SNOTEL stands for “snowpack telemetry”—basically remote data collection and transmission. Every year more than 700 remote stations like this one track snowpack and climate data across the West. That’s especially critical in Colorado.

“This is the headwater state, so this is where all of your water is coming from,” says Hultstrand, “with the exception of whatever precipitation is received in spring and summer. But really this is the bulk of it.”

The SNOTEL system has been in place for decades, transmitting data back to centralized offices where it’s assessed and compiled into reports. But it complements an even older method of data collection: manual snow surveys. From January through May, teams go out to specific sites--the same ones year after year--to collect information about snowpack. They ski or snowshoe through forests heavy with snow to the more remote sites, weathering sleet, blizzards or ice storms.

A professor at the University of Nevada named Dr. Frank Church pioneered the system of snow surveying in 1905. “He wanted to find a better way of forecasting runoff,” says Matt Hoobler, a snow surveyor with the Wyoming State Engineer's Office who conducts manual surveys each winter. And his method proved durable: during the last century, things haven’t changed much. “The basic premise of filling a tube full of snow and weighing it...we do something very similar today,” says Hoobler.

Near the summit of Interstate 80 in southeastern Wyoming, weak sunlight makes its way through the thin air to where Hoobler is taking a core sample of the snowpack. He carefully holds a specially calibrated cylinder, which measures snow depth, weight, and density, then drops it vertically into the snow, twists it, and pulls it back out to analyze.

“Water content at this site is 5.5 inches,” says Hoobler. “That means if this snow was to melt instantaneously, there’d be 5.5 inches of water sitting in this meadow.”

Matt Hoobler measures snowpack in the Rocky Mountains for river forecasting.

Hoobler collectcollects snowpack information at sites throughout Wyoming, like his colleagues in 12 other Western states. He says understanding the relationship between snowpack and water supply is critical."

“The main reason we’re up here for snow survey is for river forecasting. In other words, once it all turns to water, how much water is going to exist in our rivers and our reservoirs,” says Hoobler.

Each year surveyors add new data to the collection, validating the record and improving accuracy. The field data coming in from snow surveys and SNOTEL sites, including precipitation, is compiled into monthly stream flow forecasts for the spring and summer. That information is critical to many diverse downstream users, especially farmers who rely on irrigation water. Knowing if the water supply is above or below average can determine a boom or bust year for crops, if rancher have to sell their cows, if migrating birds will have enough water to nest successfully, if raft guides will have summer employment.

It’s generally hard for climatologists to predict what kind of a year it will be for snowpack, though global climate events like El Nino and La Nina often impact western weather. But snow accumulation is only one part of the story, since the climate of the region matters too. In 2012, some farmers ran out of their irrigation water partway through the season, even as scorching temperatures continued and rain never came. (Those that could drew heavily on the massive supply of ancient groundwater in the Ogallala Aquifer, two-thirds of which lies beneath Nebraska.) This year, irrigation demands were slow to start because of a cool, wet spring. Combined with a high snowpack runoff season, reservoirs along the North Platte River had time to fill, banking water for another dry year in the future.

As winter relaxes into spring, that massive quantity of water stored high in the mountains begins to melt and make its way into streams and rivers, where it is channeled through dams and reservoirs to feed crops. The North Platte River starts to creep north from Colorado into Wyoming, where it meets the first of several stops along the way to a farmer’s field.
