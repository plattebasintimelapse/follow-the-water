Scientists use the equation SWE = Density * Depth to approximate snow water equivalency: basically, how much water will result from the snow once it melts. Due to factors like wind, temperature and compaction over time, different types of snow have different densities. The Natural Resources Conservation Service collects and compiles this data into monthly reports. April 1st is generally the starting point in predicting spring and summer streamflow for major river systems.

Something about how unpredictable it can be

Data from the Natural Resources Conservation Service

All year, SNOTEL sites record snowpack data across the mountains of the American West. Each line in this graph represents the highest snow water equivalency measured for a few selected SNOTEL sites within the North Platte River Basin in northern Colorado for the last decade.

While sites receive different amounts of snowfall due to their location, taken together these sites demonstrate the wide variability of snowpack from year to year.

In the last decade, the North Platte River Basin has seen the highest snowpack in 30 years in 2011, followed by one of the lowest snowpack years on record in 2012. In any given year, it's difficult to predict how much snow a mountain range will receive.
