Snow water equivalency is, basically, how much water will result from snow melting all at once. Scientists use the equation SWE = Density * Depth to calculate it.

Due to factors like wind, temperature and compaction over time, different types of snow have different densities. The Natural Resources Conservation Service collects and compiles this data into monthly reports, issuing streamflow forcasts for that month.

Data from the Natural Resources Conservation Service

All year, SNOTEL sites record snowpack data across the mountains of the American West. Each line in this chart represents snow water equivalency measured for selected SNOTEL sites within the North Platte River Basin in northern Colorado for the last decade.

While sites receive different amounts of snowfall due to their location, taken together these sites demonstrate the wide variability of snowpack from year to year.

In the last decade, the North Platte River Basin has seen the highest snowpack in 30 years in 2011, followed by one of the lowest snowpack years on record in 2012. In any given year, it's difficult to predict how much snow a mountain range will receive.
